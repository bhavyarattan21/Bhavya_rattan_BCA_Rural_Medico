Curing Cancer Treatments:-

While there currently isn‚Äôt a cure for cancer, researchers are exploring several new treatments, including vaccines and gene editing, that could eventually change the face of cancer treatment.

When it comes to a cure for cancer, it‚Äôs important to understand the difference between a cure and remission:

  • Cure: A cure means that treatment has eliminated all traces of cancer from the body and has ensured that it won‚Äôt come back.
  • Remission: A person who is in remission may have few to no signs of cancer cells in their body.
    • A complete remission: no detectable signs of cancer. Often happens within the first 5 years after treatment.
    • A partial remission: the cancer has shrunk, but cancer cells are still detectable.

Even after a complete remission, cancer cells can remain and cause cancer to come back.

Newer treatments may be used alone or with conventional cancer treatments like chemotherapy and radiation therapy.

Immunotherapy

Cancer immunotherap helps the immune system fight cancer cells. Several methods help strengthen this immune response.

Vaccines

Currently, only two vaccines approved by the FDA treat cancer:

  • Bacillus Calmette-Gu√©rin (BCG): Treats early-stage bladder cancer.
  • Sipuleucel-T (Provenge): Treats metastatic prostate cancer that can't be treated by lowering testosterone levels.

Cancer vaccines usually target molecules found only on cancer cells to help the immune system identify and destroy them.

According to a 2021 review , researchers are developing new vaccines to treat various cancer types, sometimes used with other drugs.

T-cell therapy

In CAR T-cell therapy, T-cells are modified to better detect and destroy cancer cells when reintroduced into the body.

As of now, 6 CAR T-cell therapies have been approved by the FDA, including:

  • Tisagenlecleucel (Kymriah)
  • Axicabtagene ciloleucel (Yescarta)
  • Brexucabtagene autoleucel (Tecartus)
  • Idecabtagene vicleucel (Abecma)
  • Ciltacabtagene autoleucel (Carvykti)

These therapies mainly target blood cancers, like leukemia and lymphoma.

CAR T-cell therapy can cause side effects like:

  • Cytokine release syndrome (CRS)
  • Seizure-like activity
  • Confusion
  • Slurred speech

Researchers are studying ways to manage side effects and improve treatment reach.

Gene Therapy

Gene therapy treats diseases by editing or altering the genes inside your cells.

In cancer, gene therapy may fix damaged genes, replace them with healthy ones, or kill cancer cells directly.

Gene Editing

CRISPR is a gene editing tool used to target and remove faulty DNA in cancer cells. Modified DNA is then replaced to prevent tumor growth.

Early trials using CRISPR to modify T-cells in advanced cancers have shown promise, with no serious side effects reported after several months.

Virotherapy

Virotherapy uses viruses to selectively kill cancer cells. The viruses are called oncolytic viruses.

One approved virotherapy drug is talimogene laherparepvec (T-VEC), used to treat melanoma skin cancer.

Researchers continue to study different oncolytic viruses for treating cancers, aiming for better immune responses and longer-term success.
